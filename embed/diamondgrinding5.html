General Project Selection Material Construction Performance Standards/References

After showing the introduction, application, project selection of diamond grinding, this section will address the performance of diamond grinding, since it is very important both before and after the projects. Various reports stated that the field performance of diamond-ground sections is good (Caltrans, 2007) (NMDOT, 2007). Three indicators are used to evaluate performance of diamond grinding: service life extension, cost, and faulting index.

Service Life Extension

 Service life has many definitions. One study by Rao et al in 1999 shows that the average age at overlaying or reconstruction was 29.1 years and the average service life of diamond ground surfaces was 11.4 years or 10.8 million equivalent single axle loads (ESALs). But the sections in his study includes sections where diamond grinding was conducted more than once. The weather factor should also be noticed. For example, in the same study, A 225-mm (9-in.) nondoweled jointed plain concrete pavement (JPCP) in a dry climate can sustain up to 20 million ESALs after grinding before the level of faulting becomes excessive while similar pavement in a wet climate may require regrinding after 8 to 11 million ESALs. The following sections will show the life extension performance based on different criteria.

Service life based on IRI

Nationwide, studies have shown that the average longevity of a diamond ground project is around 14 years, or about 11 years at an 80% certainty (reliability) level (Rao et al. 1999). Figure 11 shows the results of 26 California Projects after diamond grinding in 2005. The bigger the IRI value before grinding, the bigger the improvement of IRI (ratio of before grinding of after grinding IRI) will be. Figure 12 shows that: 1) at 50% reliability prediction, the exponential relationship reveals that the average diamond ground PCC pavement in California will last 16.8 years when the IRI ratio equals 1.7835 which is the trigger value for rehabilitation. (Stubstad et al. 2005); 2) at 80% reliability, the extension in service life is about 14 years. The study concluded that these results were reasonable, because the climatic conditions in California were comparatively favorable for rigid pavement performance (CalTrans, 2007).

Service life based on EASL

The average service life of diamond ground pavements based on survival analysis in the study done by Rao et al. in 1999 is 37 years or 35 million ESALs since initial construction of the pavements. Many sections of the pavements have survived 40 or more years but some of these sections were diamond-ground two or more times. Further investigation is needed to conclude a more accurate number of service life based on EASL for one-time-grinding.

Cost

 Whether used alone or as part of an overall CPR program, diamond grinding costs between 2.0$ and 8.0 $ per square meter ($1.70 and $6.70 per square yard). The cost can be as high as $12/m2 or $10/y2) when concrete contains very hard river gravel. (FHWA, 2001). The history cost of diamond grinding of Michigan DOT is $4.88 (2008), $3.37 (2007), $3.49 (2006), $4.84 (2005) and have an average of $4.15 per yd2. Many state DOTs have found that the cost of diamond grinding is generally lower than the cost of an asphalt concrete overlay (Pierce 1995) (McGovern 1995). For example, Washington conducted a cost comparison of retrofit dowel bars with grinding, 1.2-m (4-ft) tied shoulders with grinding, and 110-mm (4-in.) AC overlay (Table 11) based on material costs (Pierce 1995) and found that grinding costs less than the other two methods ($69,100 per lane-km). Much of the cost savings is attributed to the fact that diamond grinding can be applied only to lanes that need the corrective treatment (FHWA, 2001). Georgia DOT found that CPR with grinding is 3 to 4 times as cost-effective as a 150-mm (6-in.) AC overlay (Rao et al. 1999).

Faulting Index Reduction

Besides the service life extension and cost analysis, Georgia DOT uses faulting index as a performance measure and has used CPR with grinding for more than 20 years. Georgia DOT experimented with the CMI Rotomill in 1976, the Klarcrete percussion device in 1978, and the Dynaplane cold planer in 1979 (Wouter 2012) and found that only diamond grinder could remove faulting without damaging the joint and did not produce an excessive noise.
