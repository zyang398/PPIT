A common pave preservation technique for portland cement concrete (PCC) is diamond grinding. The Davis Monthan Air Base in Tucson, Arizona, first used diamond grinding in 1956 to correct localized profile problems (Rao et al. 1999), and in 1965, a 19-year-old section of the San Bernardino Freeway (I-10) in California used continuous diamond grinding to eliminate excessive faulting (Neal and Woodstrom 1976). It can be used alone or with other preservation methods as a comprehensive pavement rehabilitation (CPR) program to provide a smoother ride, reduce road noise, and improve road friction (Correa and Wong 2001). Diamond grinding and diamond grooving differ in several ways. First, the purposes of these two techniques differ. While diamond grinding is mainly used to restore ride quality, improve skid resistance, and reduce tire-pavement interaction noise, diamond grooving is mainly used to produce channels to collect water and drain it on the pavement surface, thus reducing wet weather crashes with hydroplaning.
