The FHWA/SHRP Manual of Practice, Materials and Procedures for Rapid Repair of Partial-Depth Spalls in Concrete Pavements, states that premature partial-depth patch failures can be attributed to a number of material-related causes, including(Frentress & Harrington, 2012; T.P. Wilson, 1999):

1) Incompatibilities between the climatic conditions during repair replacement and the materials or procedures used.

2) Thermal incompatibility between the repair material and the pavement.

3) Extreme climatic conditions during the life of the repairs that are beyond the capabilities of the repair material.

4) Inadequate cure time prior to opening repairs to traffic.

5) Incompatibility between the joint bond breaker and the joint sealant material..

Especially in Georgia, although PDR is utilized for a long time which can be dated back to e 60s and early 70s, the patching material was identified as the root of the poor performance. It was found that some patching materials were incompatible with the thermal movement properties of the existing concrete(Gulden, 2012).

Seen from all above this choice of materials is very important. Generally, material needed for PDR depends on several factors: amount of time allowed for the repair, air temperature, size of repair, cost and estimated performance(Daniel P. Frentress, 2011; FHWA, 2005). High quality Portland cement concrete is generally most appropriate material for PDR, and it should be noted that epoxy resin can also be used and while it is in use, the manufacturer's recommendations for placement should be followed.(Daniel P. Frentress, 2011; FHWA, 2005)

Selection of proper repair mixture should be based on a determination of its desired properties such as strength of the repair mixture at a given time (e.g., when the patch needs to be opened to traffic).
