CPR techniques (ACPA, 1997)

Partial Depth Repair is used in most cases to treat spalling and surface scaling. If severe scaling is shown in a small areas, PDR can also be used(ACPA, 1998). It improves the rideability of concrete pavement, thus contributing to reduced moisture infiltration and intrusion of incompressibles into joints(ACPA, 1997 ), and also restores a uniform, well defined joint sealant reservoir prior to joint resealing (Frentress & Harrington, 2012).

For patching practices usage in US with both asphalt and concrete patching included, currently, almost all U.S. states (44 out of 49) use pavement patching except only a few states (five of 49), mainly because that the probability of having freezing temperatures in those areas is very low which means that they don’t have to consider patching to be a major component of their maintenance programs. Common procedure for Partial Depth Repair is that first identify the removal area by chain dragging and hammering the surface to find the area limits, then the removal area is marked out for further cleaning (sand blasting and air blasting). After this, concrete placement will be done to the removal area.

Partial-depth repairs usually includes 3 types: Spot repair, Long joint/crack repair and Bottom half repair. Spot repair and Long joint/crack repair are standard partial-depth repairs which is shown in Figures below. Bottom half repairs are developed for special repairs including the bottom half of the slab. Spot repairs are usually between 10 in. and 6 ft long which is typically used for pavements where the existing load transfer devices (if any) are still functional(Daniel P. Frentress, 2011). Long joint/crack repairs are usually between 6 ft and one-third to one-half the depth of the concrete pavement in transverse and longitudinal joints. Figure below is a deteriorated pavement corner that Bottom half repairs can be used. It should be noted that this repair is for work performed in the bottom half of the pavement and its use should only be applied to edges or cross-joint locations and not exceed 18 in. long (Daniel P. Frentress, 2011). For this study, we mainly focus the type 1 and type 2 PDR.
