General Project Selection Material Construction Performance Standards/References

Dilute asphalt emulsion is used for fog seal.   In the original asphalt emulsion, the percentage of water is up to 43%.   Then, an equal volume (to the original emulsion ) of water (1:1) is added to dilute the original asphalt emulsion.   If a fog seal is used for rejuvenating aged pavement surface, rejuvenating oil is also added to soften the stiffened old asphalt.  To facilitate the dilute asphalt emulsion to penetrate pavement surface, two properties are important: it should have low viscosity; and it should break slowly.  

Two types of asphalt emulsions (TxDOT, 2006) are used, cationic and anionic.   The asphalt droplets carry cationic charges in a cationic asphalt emulsion.   Similarly, they carry anionic charges in an anionic asphalt emulsion.   These two types of asphalt emulsions behave differently when asphalt breaks.   Since pavement surface is normally negatively charged, a cationic asphalt emulsion can break through the attraction between asphalt droplets and pavement surface and the loss of water due to evaporation .   For anionic asphalt emulsion, evaporation is the only way for asphalt breaking.   Thus, if all other conditions are same (humidity, pavement surface, etc.), cationic asphalt emulsion can cure faster than the anionic one.   In terms of asphalt setting time, asphalt emulsions are further classified as rapid-setting (RS), medium-setting (MS), and slow-setting (SS).   In terms of ASTM D977 and D2397, CRS, CMS, and CSS indicate cationic emulsions whereas RS, MS, and SS means anionic counterparts followed by numbers and text indicating the emulsion viscosity and residue properties.   For example, SS-1H means anionic slow-setting emulsion with low viscosity and a hard asphalt residue .   The grades of asphalt emulsion usually used in fog seals are SS-1, SS-1h, CSS-1, or CSS-1h.

For high-stress pavements, polymers are added to modify the conventional asphalt emulsions.   Polymers provide stronger bond with better long-term performance ( Muncy, 2013 ).
