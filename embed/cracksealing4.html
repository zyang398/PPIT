Weather Considerations

It is recommended that crack sealing and filling be conducted when the temperature is moderately cool (e.g., in the spring or fall season) and the crack be filled just below the top .   The reason for this is shown in the figure below, where the material remains stressed in all seasons if placed in the winter. Follow manufacturer and agency instructions and requirements specific to sealant use and ambient/surface temperature (typically 40 °F and rising) for routing and sealing.  Do not proceed if there is moisture on pavement surface or in cracks, or if rain is imminent.

Traffic Considerations

Proper traffic control plan should be developed and maintained before and during the crack sealing/filling operations.   Construct the work zone according to GDOT requirements.  Traffic should not be opened until the sealant has adequately cured and will not be picked up by vehicle tires.

Surface Preparation

Pavement surface and cracks should be dry and clean.  Repair any other pavement distresses before applying crack sealing/filling.  Use air compressor/blower to clean dust and debris before applying sealing materials.   If needed, use hot-air lance to ensure that no moisture remains on pavement surface or in cracks.

Reservoir Cutting/Routing

Routing is typically used for crack sealing operations.   A square or rectangle reservoir is cut along and centered around the crack on the pavement surface.   Follow manufacturer and/or agency requirements regarding the dimensions of the reservoir.   Clean the crack and reservoir similarly to the surface preparation procedure above.

Sealant Application

Sealant application varies according to the configuration of cracks and techniques used.   Examples of crack treatment techniques include flush fill, over-band, reservoir, and combination, and backer rod .

Follow manufacturer’s and agency’s installation temperature and configuration requirements.   Sealant should be heated and constantly remained at the minimum temperature required for pouring or application but not exceeding the material’s safe heating temperature.   Continuously agitate sealant to ensure the uniformity of the material.

Fill reservoir from the bottom up.   Finish and shape the sealant according to manufacturer’s and agency’s requirements.   Apply sufficient blotter materials (e.g., sand) on crack treatment materials to prevent wheel tracking if required to be open to traffic before materials are fully cured.

Cure time after crack sealing/filling is usually less than 1 hour (OPE, 2001). Apply a sufficient amount of blotter material to protect the uncured crack treatment material from tracking. Fine Sand (the most common used blotter), toilet paper, talcum powder, limestone dust, commercial products are often used. These blotters should be applied immediately after finishing so that they can stick to the material and serve as temporary covers. Care must be taken not to over apply dust and powder materials.
